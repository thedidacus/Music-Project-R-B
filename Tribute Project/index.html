History of R&B

The history of rhythm and blues is long and ever evolving. In the 1940s rhythm and blues was a genre of music predominantly found in African American communities. A melting pot of jazz and blues, along with instruments like pianos, drums, saxophones, bass, trumpets, and guitars. In the late 1950s through 1960s, rhythm and blues more of a "soul" theme. This would be the era where words like Motown, doo-wap, and soul music would be common when regarding rhythm and blues. Many men and women groups would emerge from this era. The focus would be heavily emphasized on vocals and harmony. In the 1970s rhythm and blue would again adopt names like funk and disco. This era would have more wacky abstract sounds, along with dancing! In the 1980s rhythm and blues would get it's first modern look with the introduction of modern equipment. Most of the instruments and media available in the 1980s are very much around today, however more advanced, available, and intricate. The 1990s would experience its own explosion of rhythm and blues. Focusing on love and expression, rhyming, blues, and the best mixing of sounds that sound has to offer, The best lyrics have to offer, this era would lead into the 2000s and be immortalized for its revolutionary style catching the world's attention. Of course music and themes of each genre had its own impacts. The political climate, cultural standards, fashion trends, global events etc. But what remains is the origins of rhythm and blues, having a long and rich background, and ever changing.

Forever My Lady by Jodeci

Forever My Lady is Jodeci's debut album released 1991. The group consisting of Dalvin (Dalvin Ertimus DeGrate), DeVante' Swing (Donald Earle DeGrate Jr.), JoJo (Joel Hailey) and K-Ci (Cedric Renard Hailey). A tremendous album with some of the most iconic songs in R&B history. The album sold over 8 million copies. Recieved the Soul Train Music Award for Best R&B/Soul Album - Group, Band or Duo in 1992.
